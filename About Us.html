When I was a kid, I was quite playful, but I somehow managed to do well in my exams. However, during my childhood, I was always interested in science and liked to perform experiments. I was also good with my hands; therefore, my parents advised me to do medicine and become a surgeon. After secondary school, I went on to the University of Lagos to study medicine, which was in 1975. My first real inspiration to become a surgeon happened during my housemanship in Lagos University Teaching Hospital (LUTH), where I had the privilege of working under professors Amaku and Esho.

They were great surgeons and that was when it clicked that this is what I wanted to do. My primary assignment during NYSC, between 1981 and 1982, in a village was near Auchi in Bendel State then (now split between Delta and Edo States), after which I went to the United States to specialize in public health and preventive medicine at Yale University.

Coincidentally, three Nigerians in my Yale set were honours students. After graduation, I went on to Columbia University for my internal medicine and cardiology residency, where another mentor, Dr. Jay Brown, an African American, gave me all the motivation I needed to become an interventional cardiologist, which was a very competitive specialty with hardly any black cardiologists.
